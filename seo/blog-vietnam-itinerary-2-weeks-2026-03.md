## Day 1-3: Hanoi — Chaos, Coffee, and the Best Food in Asia

Hanoi hits you the moment you step outside. The traffic is a wall of motorbikes, the air smells like grilled pork and exhaust fumes, and somehow it all works.

**What to do:**
- **Day 1:** Get lost in the Old Quarter. Seriously — put the map away for a few hours. Eat bun cha at a plastic-stool sidewalk spot (look for the one packed with locals). End with an egg coffee at Cafe Giang — it sounds weird, it tastes incredible.
- **Day 2:** Visit the Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum and Temple of Literature in the morning (go early, it gets hot). Afternoon: take a cooking class — you'll learn to make pho from scratch, and it'll change how you eat for the rest of the trip.
- **Day 3:** Train Street is touristy but still worth a quick visit. Then head to West Lake for a quieter side of Hanoi — rent a bike, find a lakeside cafe, watch the city slow down.

**Where to stay:** The Old Quarter is the best base. Look for boutique hotels on Ma May or Hang Bac streets — central, walkable, $25-40/night.

**Insider tip:** Hanoi's best food is on the street, not in restaurants. If a place has white tablecloths and an English menu, keep walking.

## Day 4-5: Sapa — Rice Terraces and Homestay Magic

Take the overnight sleeper train from Hanoi to Lao Cai (book a 4-berth cabin — it's an experience in itself). From there, it's a 30-minute drive up to Sapa.

**What to do:**
- **Day 4:** Trek through the Muong Hoa Valley with a Hmong guide. The rice terraces are genuinely one of the most beautiful things I've seen anywhere in the world — especially if you go between September and November when they turn gold. Stay overnight in a homestay — your host will cook dinner, and you'll eat the best rice of your life (it was grown 50 meters from the table).
- **Day 5:** Morning trek to a waterfall. Afternoon: visit the Sapa market — it's bustling and real, not staged for tourists. Evening: sleeper train back to Hanoi.

**Fitness level:** The treks are moderate — 4-6 hours of walking on uneven paths. You don't need to be an athlete, but bring decent shoes.

**Insider tip:** Skip the cable car to Fansipan unless you really want the "roof of Indochina" selfie. The real Sapa is in the valleys, not on the peak.

## Day 6-7: Ha Long Bay — But Not the Way You Think

Ha Long Bay is Vietnam's most famous sight, and honestly, it can be a letdown if you do it wrong. The tourist boats are overcrowded and follow the same route as 200 other boats.

**The GoTravel way:** We use a smaller traditional junk boat and sail to Lan Ha Bay — the quieter, equally stunning southern section that most tourists miss. Same limestone karsts, same emerald water, 10% of the crowds.

**What to do:**
- **Day 6:** Board the boat at noon, cruise through the karsts, kayak into hidden lagoons, swim off the back of the boat at sunset. Dinner is fresh seafood cooked on board.
- **Day 7:** Sunrise tai chi on the deck (optional but magical). Visit a floating fishing village, then cruise back to the mainland.

**Where to stay:** On the boat — it's an overnight cruise with private cabins.

**Insider tip:** Bring a waterproof bag for your phone. You will kayak, you will get splashed, and you will want photos.

## Day 8-9: Hue — The Quiet One Everyone Skips

Most travelers skip Hue and go straight from Hanoi to Hoi An. That's a mistake.

Hue is Vietnam's former imperial capital, and it has a gravitas that nowhere else in the country matches. It's quieter, more reflective, and the food is arguably the best in Vietnam (yes, better than Hanoi — controversial take, but I stand by it).

**What to do:**
- **Day 8:** Explore the Imperial Citadel — it's massive, partially ruined, and hauntingly beautiful. Afternoon: take a dragon boat up the Perfume River to Thien Mu Pagoda.
- **Day 9:** Morning: motorbike or bicycle through the countryside to the royal tombs (Khai Dinh is the most impressive). Afternoon: bun bo Hue crawl — the spicy beef noodle soup that this city is famous for. Try at least 3 spots. You'll have a favorite by the end.

**Getting there:** Fly from Hanoi (1 hour) or take the Reunification Express train (13 hours — scenic but long).

**Insider tip:** If you're there on a full moon night (the 14th of each lunar month), Hue lights up with lanterns along the river. Check the dates.

## Day 10-12: Hoi An — The Town That Steals Hearts

Hoi An is where most travelers say "I could live here." It's a UNESCO-listed ancient town with lantern-lit streets, tailor shops, incredible food, and a beach 15 minutes away.

**What to do:**
- **Day 10:** Walk the Ancient Town — Japanese Bridge, the Chinese assembly halls, the yellow-walled streets. Get measured for custom clothes (suits, dresses, shoes — made in 24-48 hours for a fraction of Western prices). Evening: lantern-lit boat ride on the Thu Bon River.
- **Day 11:** Morning cooking class at a riverside farm — you'll pick herbs from the garden, learn to make cao lau and white rose dumplings, and eat everything you made. Afternoon: rent a bicycle and ride to An Bang Beach.
- **Day 12:** Day trip to the My Son ruins (Vietnam's answer to Angkor Wat — smaller but uncrowded). Or: take a basket boat ride in the coconut palm forest — it's touristy and silly and incredibly fun.

**Where to stay:** Stay in the Ancient Town for atmosphere, or An Bang Beach for a quieter vibe. Both are excellent.

**Insider tip:** For tailoring, go to a shop on the side streets — not the main tourist drag. Quality is the same, prices are 30-40% lower. Get fitted on day 1 so you have time for adjustments.

---

## Day 13-14: Ho Chi Minh City — Energy, History, and Street Food

Vietnam's southern capital is the opposite of Hanoi — louder, faster, more modern, more chaotic. It's a city that moves at motorbike speed.

**What to do:**
- **Day 13:** Morning: Cu Chi Tunnels — the underground network used during the war. It's sobering and fascinating, especially with a veteran guide who can add personal context. Afternoon: War Remnants Museum (prepare to be emotionally wrecked — it's important). Evening: rooftop drinks overlooking the skyline, then street food in District 4 (banh mi, banh xeo, and fresh spring rolls).
- **Day 14:** Ben Thanh Market in the morning (for souvenirs — haggle hard). Then wander through Chinatown (District 5) for dim sum and temple visits. If time allows, explore the cafe scene — Saigon's specialty coffee culture is booming.

**Where to stay:** District 1 for convenience, District 3 for a more local feel at lower prices.

**Insider tip:** Don't take a taxi from the airport — use Grab (Vietnam's Uber). It's 70% cheaper and you won't get scammed on the meter.

## Practical Tips for Your 2-Week Vietnam Trip

- **Budget:** $40-60/day for mid-range travel (accommodation, food, local transport, activities). Domestic flights are $30-80 if booked in advance.
- **Visa:** Most nationalities now get 45 days visa-free (as of 2025). Check the latest rules for your passport.
- **Best time to go:** October-April for the north, December-April for the south. Avoid Tet (Vietnamese New Year, late January/early February) unless you want everything closed.
- **Money:** Vietnamese Dong (VND). ATMs are everywhere. Cards accepted in cities but carry cash for markets and small towns.
- **SIM card:** Buy one at the airport for $5-10 — unlimited data, works everywhere.
- **Language:** Learn "xin chao" (hello) and "cam on" (thank you). Google Translate works surprisingly well for the rest.

### Is Vietnam safe for solo travelers?
Yes — Vietnam is one of the safest countries in Southeast Asia for solo travelers, including women traveling alone. Petty theft (bag snatching on motorbikes) happens in cities, so keep valuables close. Violent crime against tourists is extremely rare.
