# PAGES 8–10 — Smart Spending Guide

## The Smart Spending Guide: Where to Save, Where to Spend

Not all spending decisions are equal. Here's exactly where your money makes the biggest difference — and where it doesn't.

---

### Save Here

**Accommodation in cities (most nights)**
For a base to sleep, shower, and store your bags between adventures, a $15–25 guesthouse is completely fine. The city is your experience — not the room.

**Meals 80% of the time**
Eat where locals eat. Street stalls, market canteens, and tiny family restaurants with no English menu are where the best food lives — and a full meal rarely tops $3. Save restaurants for one or two special evenings.

**Internal city transport**
Grab (the regional Uber) is reliable, safe, and priced fairly. Walking is free and often more interesting. Renting a bicycle costs $3–5/day in most cities and is the best way to explore at your own pace.

**Packaged tours at major sites**
For standard day trips to common attractions, local tour operators in each city are significantly cheaper than hotel-booked options — 20–40% less for the same tour. Ask at your guesthouse which local agency they recommend (not which one they're affiliated with).

---

### Spend Here

**Overnight transport**
An overnight sleeper bus or train for $15–30 gets you between cities while you sleep — saving both transport cost and a night's accommodation. This is the single best value decision in Southeast Asia travel.

**A quality Ha Long Bay cruise**
The difference between a $50/night budget boat and a $150/night mid-range junk isn't the boat — it's the food, the crowd, and whether you feel like you're in a floating paradise or a floating queue. Spend the money.

**Local, guided experiences**
Cooking classes ($20–40), guided market tours ($15–25), village treks with local guides ($25–50) — these are the moments you'll describe for years. They're also surprisingly affordable.

**Travel insurance**
We've said it before. We'll say it again. SafetyWing starts at $42/month. One medical incident without coverage can cost $3,000–15,000. This is not optional.

**Good guides for major historical sites**
Angkor Wat, the Cu Chi Tunnels, the WWII sites in Kanchanaburi — these places need context. A licensed guide ($15–30 for a half-day) transforms a walk among ancient stones into something you'll never forget.

---

### The "Just This Once" Trap

You'll encounter it at every turn: the $6 cocktail at the beautiful rooftop bar, the $25 cooking class at the upscale resort, the $40 speedboat because it's an hour faster than the $8 slow ferry. None of these are bad choices individually.

The trap is when "just this once" happens seven times in a day. Budget a daily "spontaneous spending" allowance — $10–20 — and let yourself enjoy it freely. Once it's gone, it's gone. You'll enjoy the cocktail more when it's a choice, not a habit.

# PAGE 11 — The Money-Saving Packing List

## Pack This, Save Money

What you bring directly affects what you spend. These 15 items will save you money every single day.

**Documents & Money**
- [ ] **Wise or Revolut debit card** — zero foreign transaction fees, real exchange rates. Load it before you leave.
- [ ] **Physical copies of all documents** — passport, visa, insurance. Stored separately from originals.
- [ ] **Travel insurance documents** — printed and saved offline on your phone. Know your emergency number.

**Health & Hygiene**
- [ ] **Prescription medications + a small first-aid kit** — basic pharmacy items cost 3–5x more in tourist areas
- [ ] **Reef-safe sunscreen (SPF 50)** — quality sunscreen is expensive to buy in Southeast Asia, especially on islands
- [ ] **Electrolyte packets** — heat + movement = you'll need these. Buy in bulk before you go (~$15 for 30 packets vs. $3–5 each at local pharmacies)
- [ ] **Small padlock** — for locker rooms, guesthouse lockers, and bag security on overnight transport

**Tech**
- [ ] **Universal travel adapter** — Southeast Asia uses multiple socket types. One adapter covers everything.
- [ ] **Portable power bank (20,000mAh)** — for long days and overnight transport. Saves you paying for charge stations.
- [ ] **Offline maps downloaded** — Google Maps offline or Maps.me for your countries. Navigation without data.
- [ ] **Translation app downloaded offline** — Google Translate offline packs for Vietnamese, Thai, Khmer. Life-changing in local markets.

**Clothing & Gear**
- [ ] **Quick-dry travel towel** — guesthouses often charge $1–3 for towel rental or don't provide them
- [ ] **Lightweight rain jacket or poncho** — buying one in a tropical rainstorm at a tourist stall costs 3–5x more
- [ ] **Reef shoes or water sandals** — essential for island activities; cheap versions sold locally are uncomfortable and fall apart
- [ ] **Sarong or lightweight scarf** — temple dress code (shoulders and knees covered) required throughout the region. Buy one, use it everywhere. Don't rent or buy repeatedly at temple entrances.
